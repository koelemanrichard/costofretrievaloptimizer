Based on the Koray Framework and the provided sources, the assumption that Semantic SEO requires "always very large content" is **incorrect**. In fact, writing unnecessarily long content increases the **Cost of Retrieval (CoR)** for search engines, which can harm your rankings.

The guiding principle is: **"Content should be as short as possible and as long as necessary"**,.

Here is the complete, detailed breakdown of rules, logic, and actionable strategies for determining content length, specifically focusing on when and how to write shorter content.

### **I. The Core Philosophy: Cost of Retrieval vs. Information Density**

Search engines operate on a budget (crawling and processing resources). Your goal is to provide the maximum amount of unique information with the minimum amount of text (tokens) and code.

* **Information Density Rule:** You must increase the value per sentence. If you can explain a concept in 3 sentences instead of 3 paragraphs, the shorter version ranks higher because it is "cheaper" for the search engine to process,.  
* **Cost of Retrieval (CoR):** If your website quality is 8/10 but your cost (length/complexity) is 9/10, you are too expensive to rank. A shorter, denser article is preferred over a long, fluffy one,.  
* **Length Normalization:** Algorithms compare your length to the average. If competitors are long but "fluffy," a short, dense article wins. However, if the topic *requires* depth (e.g., "History of WWII"), being too short will signal a lack of authority.

### **II. When to Write Shorter Content (Specific Use Cases)**

You should write shorter content in the following scenarios. The "Short" definition here relies on covering the *necessary* attributes without expanding into unrelated contexts.

#### **1\. Boolean Queries (Yes/No Questions)**

* **Context:** Queries asking "Can," "Is," "Does." (e.g., "Can cats eat olives?").  
* **Rule:** The answer must be immediate. Do not write a 2,000-word backstory.  
* **Action:** Start with a direct "Yes" or "No" in the first sentence. Explain the *condition* (e.g., "Yes, but only in small amounts") immediately after.  
* **Good Example:**  
  * *Query:* "Is hot water good to drink?"  
  * *Content:* "Yes, hot water is good to drink. It aids digestion and improves circulation..." (Direct, short).  
* **Bad Example:**  
  * *Content:* "Water has been essential for life for millions of years. Scientists have studied temperature..." (Fluff, delays the answer),.

#### **2\. Definition/Glossary Terms (Micro-Contexts)**

* **Context:** "What is \[X\]?" queries located in the **Supplementary** or **Outer** sections of your Topical Map.  
* **Rule:** Define the entity clearly. Link it back to the Core Section. Stop writing once the definition is complete.  
* **Action:** Do not expand into "History of \[X\]" or "Types of \[X\]" unless the query specifically demands it.  
* **Length:** Often 300-500 words is sufficient if it covers the definition and immediate attributes,.

#### **3\. Low Search Volume / Bridge Topics**

* **Context:** Topics with zero or very low search volume that are necessary to connect two high-volume topics in your map (Contextual Bridges).  
* **Rule:** These do not need to be "Quality Nodes" (massive guides). They just need to exist to complete the semantic network.  
* **Action:** Write concise content (e.g., 200â€“400 words) that satisfies the logical connection between Entity A and Entity B.

#### **4\. Disambiguation Pages**

* **Context:** When a term has multiple meanings (e.g., "Apple" the fruit vs. "Apple" the brand).  
* **Rule:** Quickly differentiate the meanings and provide links to the respective deep content. Keep the text minimal.

### **III. When to Write Longer Content (Quality Nodes)**

Long content is required *only* when you are creating a **Quality Node** (a Centerpiece content item) intended to prove ultimate authority.

* **Root Attributes:** If the entity is a "Country" (e.g., Germany), you must cover huge attributes like Population, History, Economy, and Geography. This naturally results in long content,.  
* **Core Section (Monetization):** The pages that make money (e.g., "Best SEO Software") usually require covering every possible user criterion (Price, Features, Support, Speed), resulting in longer content to satisfy "Information Gain".  
* **Outranking High-Authority Competitors:** If a competitor covers 50 attributes, you may need to cover 60 to win. This increases length, but only if the additional length provides *new* information (Unique Information Gain).

---

### **IV. Rules for Reducing Length & Increasing Density (Algorithmic Authorship)**

To write shorter but higher-ranking content, apply these specific editing rules from the framework:

1. **Remove "Also":** Delete the word "also" from every sentence. It usually adds no meaning.  
2. **Remove Modality (Uncertainty):** Change "X might help Y" to "X helps Y." Removing modal verbs (can, could, might, may) shortens the sentence and increases the factual confidence score,.  
3. **Strict Sentence Structure:** Use "Subject \+ Predicate \+ Object." Avoid nested, complex sentences that confuse the parser.  
   * *Bad:* "The car, which was red and fast, won the race."  
   * *Good:* "The red, fast car won the race.".  
4. **No Fluff/Analogies:** Do not use analogies (e.g., "SEO is like a garden..."). Search engines treat this as noise/gibberish. State the fact directly,.  
5. **Stop at the Topical Border:** If you are writing about "Electric Bikes," do not write 500 words about "How Electricity Works." That belongs on a different page. Cutting off at the "Topical Border" keeps content short and relevant,.

### **V. Technical & Structural Limits**

Regardless of word count, these technical limits dictate the "size" of your content in the eyes of a search engine.

* **The 400-Character Rule (Centerpiece Annotation):** The most important information (the direct answer to the user's query) must appear within the first **400 characters** of the main content. If you delay the answer with fluff, the engine may assume the rest of the page is irrelevant,.  
* **HTML Size Limit:**  
  * **Ideal:** Keep raw HTML under **125 KB**.  
  * **Maximum:** Never exceed **450 KB**.  
  * If your text makes the HTML larger than this, Googlebot may stop crawling (truncation), and the bottom of your content will be ignored,.  
* **DOM Nodes:** Keep DOM nodes under 1,500 (ideally under 600). Long content often bloats DOM size; keeping content concise helps technical performance.

### **VI. Summary Checklist: Determining Length**

Use this decision matrix for every article:

| Content Goal | Strategy | Length Requirement | Source |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Answer a specific question** | **Centerpiece Annotation:** Put the answer in the first paragraph. | **Short.** Stop after answering and providing evidence. |  |
| **Bridge two topics** | **Contextual Bridge:** Justify the link between Topic A and B. | **Short.** Only long enough to define the relationship. |  |
| **Core Monetization Page** | **Comprehensive Coverage:** Cover every attribute competitors cover \+ unique ones. | **Long.** Must be detailed to prove authority. |  |
| **Listicle (e.g., "Best X")** | **Item Count:** Depends on the number of items. | **Variable.** Short descriptions per item are better than long fluff. |  |
| **News/Updates** | **Freshness:** State the new fact immediately. | **Short.** Focus on the "new" delta. |  |

**Final Rule:** Never add a sentence just to reach a word count target. If a tool tells you to write 2,000 words, but you have covered every fact in 800 words, **publish the 800-word version**. It has higher information density and lower cost of retrieval,.
